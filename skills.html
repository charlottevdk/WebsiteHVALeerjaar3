Critical Thinking

Moving beyond describing what happened to explaining why it happened — and what it means for what comes next. Critical thinking at HvA CE means identifying patterns in data, challenging assumptions, and turning analysis into recommendation.

Project 02 — Market Intelligence

From “what” to “why” and “what's next”

The MIS Foot & Orthognathic report was not a data dump — it was a structured argument. I moved from procedure volume data through competitive positioning to a portfolio gap assessment that revealed Small Bone instruments as Stryker's most underutilised segment relative to market potential.

This required cross-referencing internal sales figures against external market data, questioning why a seemingly minor segment had outsized commercial upside, and formulating a clear strategic recommendation — not just a description of what I found.

Portfolio AnalysisData SynthesisStrategic Recommendation
Project 03 — Jetpak Audit

Root cause analysis under ambiguity

When only 6 of 26 banners were found, the easy answer was “assets are missing.” The critical answer asked: why do assets go missing systematically? I identified three root causes — no traceability, outdated logs, no governance owner — and built a recommendations framework from those causes rather than just documenting the symptom.

This demonstrated the ability to think structurally about a problem rather than reactively about its surface, and to link analysis directly to actionable change.

Root Cause AnalysisStructural ThinkingProcess Design

Teamwork

Contributing to shared goals across diverse teams, taking initiative, supporting others, and managing stakeholder relationships — including upward and cross-functional. Teamwork in CE means being someone others want on the project.

Project 01 — LVO Congress

7-stakeholder coordination

The LVO Congress required coordinating seven internal and external stakeholders: Stryker colleagues, the venue team at Gooiland Hilversum, and third-party logistics and materials suppliers. I was the central point of contact — drafting communications, chasing confirmations, resolving conflicts in schedule, and keeping everyone aligned on the day-of plan.

No delays. No issues on either congress day. The feedback from colleagues reflected a smooth, professional execution they could rely on.

Stakeholder ManagementCross-functional Coordination
Projects 03 & 05 — Jetpak & OI Academy

Contributing as a team member

At Jetpak, I worked alongside Nazli Tiryaki and Hande Cankaya Keles in a structured three-person audit team — distributing tasks, sharing observations in real time, and synthesising findings collectively before writing up the report.

At OI Academy 201, I participated as a colleague alongside sales reps with years more experience. I contributed to group exercises, asked questions that helped the wider group, and built relationships with team members across countries.

Cross-Cultural CollaborationField Teamwork

Commercial Awareness

Understanding how markets, customers, and business models interact — and applying that understanding to make commercially sound decisions. In practice: seeing revenue implications, identifying where value is being left on the table, and connecting marketing activity to business outcomes.

Project 01 — LVO Congress

ROI-first event strategy

Rather than treating the LVO Congress as a logistics exercise, I approached it as a commercial question: what return is Stryker generating from congress attendance, and how do we measure it? The ROI analysis framework I built connected event cost to leads generated, giving the team a methodology they could apply to future event decisions.

The 5-congress landscape analysis added a second layer: prioritising which events deserve investment based on audience quality and commercial history — a direct application of commercial thinking to marketing spend.

ROI AnalysisMarketing Spend Prioritisation
Project 02 — Market Intelligence

Portfolio gap as commercial opportunity

Identifying that Small Bone instruments were underutilised relative to Benelux procedure volumes was not just a research finding — it was a commercial signal. The insight pointed to a potential revenue gap: a segment where Stryker has product capability but insufficient market penetration.

Framing the gap as a commercial opportunity rather than a product catalogue observation required understanding how procedure volumes translate to sales potential — the kind of commercial reasoning that connects market data to revenue growth.

Market Opportunity AnalysisPortfolio Strategy

Aanpassingsvermogen

Adaptability

Functioning effectively outside your comfort zone — whether that means taking on a technically demanding assignment, working across cultures, or proactively reshaping how you work in response to new tools and circumstances.

Project 04 — Copilot AI

Self-directed adoption of new technology

The Copilot initiative began without a brief, a manager request, or a formal project scope. I identified a tool, taught myself how to use it effectively, built use cases from scratch, and then developed the confidence to present it to a 5-country team — all within the span of a few weeks.

This was adaptability not as a reaction to change, but as a deliberate choice to get ahead of it. Recognising that AI was going to change how commercial teams work, and deciding to be the person who helped Stryker adapt rather than waiting to be told to.

Self-Directed LearningAI AdoptionInitiative
Project 05 — OI Academy 201

Succeeding in a technical domain outside my background

As the only non-sales, non-clinical participant in OI Academy 201, I had to adapt my learning approach — no textbook preparation, no prior exposure to surgical anatomy, and a room full of colleagues with years of product experience. I passed with 80%, meeting the threshold, by committing to four full days of focused engagement and finding ways to anchor clinical concepts to what I already knew from market research.

The experience also adapted my perspective: I came out with a materially different understanding of what Stryker sells and why it matters.

Out-of-Comfort-Zone LearningClinical Domain

Communication

Crafting and delivering the right message to the right audience in the right format — whether that is a stakeholder email, a live demo to a multinational team, a written report, or a visual training document. Communication is both written and spoken, internal and external.

Project 04 — Copilot AI

Cross-country live presentation

Presenting a new technology tool to a team spread across five countries required adapting the message for a mixed audience — some tech-comfortable, some sceptical, some time-pressured. I structured the presentation around the “15-minute gift” concept: a concrete, tangible outcome that cut through the noise of abstract AI hype.

The Cheat Sheet I created after the presentation was a different communication challenge — distilling complex tool capabilities into a one-page reference a rep could use without guidance. Design, language, and hierarchy all had to work for a busy commercial audience.

Multinational PresentationKnowledge Documentation
Projects 01 & 03 — LVO & Jetpak

Written communication in high-stakes contexts

For LVO, I wrote stakeholder communications that needed to be clear, professional, and action-oriented — coordinating seven parties through a logistics chain with little margin for ambiguity. Colleagues responded positively to the structure and tone of my written outreach.

For Jetpak, the audit findings report had to communicate sensitive operational gaps without creating blame or defensiveness — a communication challenge that required framing, evidence, and recommendation in a sequence that built trust rather than triggering defensiveness. The response from Juha Soininen confirmed the report was well-received.
