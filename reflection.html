Who I am becoming
as a professional.

Six months at Stryker gave me more than project results. It gave me a clearer sense of the work I want to do, the environments I thrive in, and the professional I'm becoming.

Before Stryker, I knew I liked marketing. I was less certain about which type, for which organisation, in which market. The internship answered that in a way six months of lectures couldn't.

MedTech combines the two things I find most motivating: analytical rigour and patient impact. The decisions Stryker makes about which instruments to develop, how to position them, and which procedures to target have real consequences for surgical outcomes. That proximity to purpose is not something I want to give up.

I came in thinking I might go into consumer marketing after graduating. I leave certain I want to stay in healthcare or medtech, where the stakes make the rigour worthwhile.

Key insight
Purpose and commercial rigour reinforce each other in MedTech. Good marketing helps better instruments reach more surgeons, which improves patient outcomes. That alignment is what makes this sector feel meaningful to me.

Not all marketing work is the same. I have done execution, analysis, and creative communication during my internship — and I now know I add the most value at the intersection of analysis and communication.

I'm energised by projects where I synthesise information into a clear narrative — an intelligence report, an event ROI analysis, a Copilot use case for a commercial team. At Stryker, Juha gave me ownership over projects rather than tasks within someone else's project. That model brings the best out of me.

What I've learned about myself
I need to understand the “why” behind what I'm doing. When I understand the commercial or strategic purpose of a project, I naturally invest more and produce better work. Going forward, I'll proactively seek that context — and offer it to others.

Stryker is a multinational with deep data infrastructure, a structured performance culture, and a genuinely warm team dynamic. Those three things together are rarer than they sound.

Working within a 5-country Northern Europe team meant constant cross-cultural exposure — different communication styles, different relationships to hierarchy. That was sometimes uncomfortable, but mostly exhilarating. I want to keep working internationally.

Juha gave me full ownership of my projects from day one: set expectations, made himself available, let me take responsibility for the quality of output. That approach — stretching, trusting, then recognising the result — is what I want from a future employer and what I'll eventually want to offer as a manager myself.

What I take forward
The bar. I now know what good looks like — how a high-performing commercial team operates, what it means to own a project professionally, and the standard of output that earns respect in a demanding business environment.
Areas for Growth

Where I want to develop further

Honest reflection means naming the gaps as clearly as the strengths.

01
Advanced Analytics — Power BI & SQL
My analysis work relied on Excel and qualitative synthesis. Power BI and SQL would have made my intelligence work faster and more visually compelling. I'm actively learning these — I want my next role to accelerate that development in a data environment where dashboards are daily work.
02
Senior Management Presentations
I presented to my direct team with confidence. Presenting to C-suite — limited time, high stakes, sceptical audience — is a different skill. I want to put myself in those rooms earlier in my career rather than later, and I'll actively seek opportunities to present upward.
03
Cross-Cultural Communication Depth
The 5-country team gave me exposure, but mostly in English. Building fluency in reading cultural signals more subtly — especially in written communication — is something that only comes from time spent working closely with diverse teams over years.
04
Regulatory Knowledge — MDR & CE Marking
The EU Medical Device Regulation framework and CE marking are fundamental to how Stryker markets in Europe. I encountered this context regularly but never went deep. Understanding how regulatory constraints shape commercial decisions in MedTech is a genuine advantage for a marketing professional in this sector.
Looking Ahead

Where I'm going from here

My goal after graduating is to secure a graduate role or internship at an international, data-driven organisation — preferably in MedTech, life sciences, or a similarly complex B2B sector where marketing requires genuine analytical depth alongside communicative skill.

I'm not looking for a generalist marketing role. I want an environment where I'm surrounded by people who are excellent at what they do, where data informs decisions, and where I have real ownership of work that matters.

Stryker gave me a blueprint. I now know what I'm looking for — and what I have to offer. The combination of analytical thinking, commercial awareness, communication, and adaptability is not common in a graduate. I want to find the organisation that makes the most of it.

Organisation type
International, data-driven, MedTech or complex B2B. Performance culture that also values people.
Role characteristics
Project ownership early on. Analytical and communicative in equal measure. A manager who leads by trusting, not controlling.
Development priorities
Power BI and SQL. Senior stakeholder exposure. International team dynamics. Regulatory understanding in healthcare.
Longer-term ambition
A commercial leadership role at a company where the product genuinely improves lives. Purpose and performance — both non-negotiable.
