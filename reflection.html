Industry Fit

Why MedTech is the right field

Before Stryker, I knew I liked marketing. I was less certain about which type of marketing, for which kind of organisation, in which kind of market. The internship answered that question in a way six months of lectures could not.

MedTech combines the two things I find most motivating: analytical rigour and patient impact. The decisions a company like Stryker makes about which instruments to develop, how to position them, and which procedures to target have real consequences for surgical outcomes. That proximity to purpose is not something I want to give up.

At the same time, MedTech is a commercially demanding environment. You need to understand clinical evidence, competitive dynamics, procedure volumes, and budget cycles simultaneously. That complexity — far from putting me off — is exactly what makes the work interesting. The MIS Foot market intelligence report showed me what it looks like to do that kind of thinking well.

I came in thinking I might go into consumer marketing after graduating. I leave certain that I want to stay in healthcare or medtech, where the stakes make the rigour worthwhile.

Key Insight
Purpose and commercial rigour are not in tension in MedTech — they reinforce each other. Good marketing helps better instruments reach more surgeons, which improves patient outcomes. That alignment between commercial success and clinical value is what makes this sector feel meaningful to me.
What confirmed it
OI Academy 201 gave me a direct view of the clinical environment Stryker sells into. Sitting in a room learning about surgical anatomy and instrument technique gave me a visceral sense of what is at stake when a product works — or doesn't. I didn't expect that experience to affect me, but it did.
Work Type

The kind of work I do best

Not all marketing work is the same. Some of it is execution — producing content, managing calendars, routing approvals. Some of it is analytical — reading markets, identifying patterns, building frameworks for decisions. Some of it is creative — finding the right message for the right audience.

I have done all three during my internship, and I now have a much clearer sense of where I add the most value: at the intersection of analysis and communication. I am energised by projects where I have to synthesise information into a clear narrative — whether that is a market intelligence report, an event ROI analysis, or a Copilot use case for a commercial team.

I am less energised by pure administration and repetitive execution tasks. This isn't a refusal to do them — it's an honest recognition of where I produce my best work and where I grow the most. At Stryker, I was fortunate to have a manager who gave me ownership over projects rather than just assigning tasks within someone else's project. That model brings the best out of me.

Where I thrive
Projects with a clear problem to solve, latitude to determine the approach, and a meaningful deliverable at the end. The MIS Foot report, the Jetpak audit, the Copilot initiative — all three had these qualities, and all three produced work I'm proud of.
What I've learned about myself
I need to understand the “why” behind what I'm doing. When I understand the commercial or strategic purpose of a project, I naturally invest more, push further, and produce better work. When that context is absent, I'm less effective. Going forward, I will proactively seek out that context — and offer it to others.
Work Environment

The kind of company I want to grow in

Stryker is a multinational with deep data infrastructure, a structured performance culture, and a genuinely warm team dynamic. Those three things together are rarer than they sound. Many large organisations have the structure without the warmth, or the warmth without the ambition. Stryker had all three.

Working within a 5-country Northern Europe team meant constant cross-cultural exposure — different communication styles, different relationships to hierarchy, different paces of work. That was sometimes uncomfortable, but mostly exhilarating. I want to keep working internationally.

Critically, my manager Juha Soininen gave me full ownership of my projects from day one. He did not over-manage or micromanage. He set expectations, made himself available, and let me take responsibility for the quality of my output. That approach — stretching, trusting, and then recognising the result — is what I want from a future employer. It is also, I think, what I will eventually want to offer as a manager myself.

I want to work for an organisation that is data-driven, internationally oriented, and commercially ambitious — but that has not sacrificed its people culture in the process. Stryker showed me that combination exists.

What I need from a workplace
Autonomy over meaningful projects, a team I can learn from, and an environment that values rigour. I function best when I am trusted to determine the approach rather than given a checklist to follow.
What Stryker gave me
A manager who championed my initiative. Colleagues who treated me as a peer rather than an intern. Projects that mattered to the business. The combination made me a better professional and a more confident one.
What I take forward
The bar. I now know what good looks like — how a high-performing commercial team operates, what it means to own a project professionally, and the standard of output that earns respect in a demanding business environment.
Areas for Growth

Where I want to develop further

Honest reflection means naming the gaps as clearly as the strengths. These four areas are where I know I have room to grow — and where I am actively investing.

01
Advanced Data Analytics — Power BI & SQL
My analysis work at Stryker relied on Excel and qualitative synthesis. I could see throughout the internship that Power BI and SQL skills would have made my market intelligence work faster and more visually compelling. These are tools I am actively learning, and I want my next role to accelerate that development — working in a data environment where dashboards and queries are part of daily work, not a specialist function.
02
Senior Management Presentations
I presented the Copilot initiative and several project findings to my direct team with confidence. Presenting to C-suite or senior management — in a setting where time is limited, stakes are high, and the audience is sceptical — is a different skill. I want to put myself in those rooms earlier in my career rather than later, and I need to actively seek opportunities to present upward, not just laterally.
03
Cross-Cultural Communication Depth
Working across a 5-country Northern Europe team gave me exposure to different communication styles, but I was mostly communicating in English — my strongest language. Building fluency in reading cultural signals more subtly, particularly in written communication, is an area I want to deepen. This is not a skill that can be learned from a course; it comes from time spent working closely with diverse teams.
04
Regulatory & Clinical Knowledge (MDR / CE Marking)
The Medical Device Regulation (MDR) framework and CE marking requirements are fundamental to how Stryker markets its products in Europe. During my internship, I encountered this context regularly but never dove deep into it. Understanding how regulatory constraints shape commercial and marketing decisions in MedTech is a genuine competitive advantage for a marketing professional in this sector — and something I want to develop as I continue in the field.
Looking Ahead

Where I am going from here

My goal after graduating is to secure a graduate internship or entry-level role at an international, data-driven organisation — preferably in MedTech, life sciences, or a similarly complex B2B sector where marketing requires genuine analytical depth alongside creative and communicative skill.

I am not looking for a generalist marketing role. I want to be in an environment where I am surrounded by people who are excellent at what they do, where data informs decisions rather than decorates them, and where I have real ownership of work that matters.

Stryker gave me a blueprint. I now know what I am looking for — and more importantly, I know what I have to offer. The combination of analytical thinking, commercial awareness, strong communication, and the adaptability to operate across domains is not common in a graduate. I want to find the organisation that will make the most of it.

Organisation Type
International, data-driven, with genuine product complexity — ideally MedTech, life sciences, or comparable B2B sector. Must have a performance culture that also values people.
Role Characteristics
Project ownership from early on. Analytical and communicative in equal measure. Cross-functional exposure. A manager who leads by trusting and stretching, not by controlling.
Development Priorities
Power BI and SQL capability. Senior stakeholder exposure. International team dynamics. Regulatory market understanding in healthcare sectors.
Longer-Term Ambition
A commercial leadership role — marketing director or commercial strategy — at a company where the product genuinely improves lives. The combination of purpose and performance is non-negotiable.
